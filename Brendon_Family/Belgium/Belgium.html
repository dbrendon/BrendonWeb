Belgium

Kerala's Winter Break

Once again we took advantage of one of David's business trips. He was to be in Roermond, The Netherlands (oh heck if you don't mind we're going to just call it Holland as we are on the mainland (or should we say the main landfill) however Dutch people in the south would correct you to say that you're not in Holland but rather in Limburg. I've never known what to say; The Netherlands vs Holland and then they don't speak Hollandish it's Dutch, nor are their citizens Netherlandians.... We suppose it's the same confusion when it comes to the United Kingdom/Great Britain/England who speak English). Anyway we made use of the two weekends on either end of the business trip and checked out Belgium on the first and Holland on the second.

Friday, February 7:

Friday evening immediately after work David picked up the rental car from downtown Copenhagen. We had originally thought that we'd leave around 6:00 pm but then figured that if we drove all through the night we'd arrive in Brussels too early in the morning so we had dinner at home and left around 8:30 pm. It had started to lightly snow when we started off. 45 minutes into the drive we approached our nemesis, the bridge connecting the island of Zealand (which we live on) to the next island, Funen, which will lead you onto the mainland of Jutland which is connected to the rest of Europe. The cost of the bridge is a whopping 245 Kr which is $54.96 Cdn and that's just one way. With some fun trying to get a receipt and our usual complaining about the cost we were on our way. Along the highway there are speed control signs which show Your Speed XXX km/hr (Din Fart XXX km/hr).

It takes about 3 hours to get out of Denmark into Germany. We didn't hit any border checks; however you can see the remains of the border crossing buildings (similar to the ones between the US and Canada) that would have been operating pre-European Union. It was a drizzly, foggy night. Kerala was able to sleep for most of the drive. She had a sleeping bag for a blanket, a neck pillow and wore the fancy schmancy seat belt pad thing-a-majig that her Nana and Papa sent over. We had her get out for a washroom break and stretch around 1:30 am and 4:00 am. Kerala and Mommy had developed a very original stretching routine which was second only to the man who used the gas pump for his stretches???

Once we hit Germany it was "Fun Fun Fun on the Autobahn" - (David's memory of up north at his Great Uncle Bert's cottage on Lake Simcoe with his cousin Michael picking up this song with the transistor radio his Nana and Papa had given him.) Brenda was quite surprised to see that the Autobahn is just a regular highway - identical to at home. As a child when her neighbours the Oswalds returned from a trip to Europe they had told her about this highway that you could go as fast as you liked. She had envisioned this strip of concrete with sloping barriers on either side - something like a speed way, but nope it's just a normal highway like home - except the exits all read "ausfart". There are many rest areas along the way and service centres just like back home, however there's an attendant for the washrooms (not there in the wee - no pun intended - hours of the morning). A man or woman wearing a white jacket stands outside the washrooms and there's a wicker basket with coins in it just outside the doors which you are to "contribute"/pay to. Another difference is the size of the cappuccinos. A ritual for when we change drivers (our experience of all nighters down to Florida and/or Cape Hatteras, North Carolina) from David to Brenda is to fill up on gas and purchase a large cappuccino which Brenda will nurse for the first hour of the drive. For €2.50 ($3.95) Cdn the cappuccino was so small it didn't even last long enough to get her out of the parking lot.

David took the 8:30 pm to 1:30 am shift, Brenda 1:50 am to 4:00 am, David 4:00 am to 6:00 am and Brenda 6:00 am to 10:00 am. The signs take a little getting used to. Back home we read the signs from top to bottom so that the first place you will reach will be at the top of the sign, the next place listed second, and so on. In Germany it is the reverse. So now it was our chance to make up time and take advantage of the limitless speed. Watching the signage is very important. There were stretches of the Autobahn which were 80 km/hr, 100 km/hr or 120 km/hr. We can't figure out what is so "special" about those areas as the road seemed to be flat and very few entrances? not sure of the logic. When the lower speed limit ends the sign will simply have the former speed limit posted with a line crossed through it. We found the car we had worked well up until 130 km/hr but between 130 and 140 it seemed as though there should be a 6th gear. How many times Brenda went to change gears to find we were at the top one. As a result we traveled most of the time just above 140. However Brenda was intrigued and during her first shift while David & Kerala were asleep waited for a clear stretch and took the car up to 160 km/hr. The sign posts seemed to fly by - a blur. It was amazing how you'd be going over 140 km/hr and a car out of no where would whiz by you and their tail lights would be out of sight in no time. Once again we're reminded based on driving on American highways and here in Europe that Canadians just don't seem to get it - traffic runs much more smoothly if the left hand lane is used only for passing.

Finally around 10:00 am after a few attempts to find the entrance (Lonely Planet warned us about Belgian drivers and we experienced it in our attempt to go across 5 lanes of a one way street) we managed to claim one of the 5 free parking spots of the Crowne Royal Hotel in Brussels. We freshened up and began our day of touring around Brussels.

Brussels

Saturday, February 8:

When making our plans for this trip we weren't too sure if Brussels was to be on our list of places to visit. However we were curious to see this place that you always hear of news correspondents reporting from when reporting on the EU and NATO. So here we are walking to downtown Brussels passing many of the very distinctive EU flags questioning why Germany's black, yellow and red striped flag was flying by its side. Little did we know that it was the flag of Belgium same colour of the stripes but different order and horizontal. What we had thought was the flag of Belgium, the yellow with black lion, is that of Flanders. Oops. Sort of like mistaking the Fleur de Lis Quebec flag as the flag of Canada. And we thought we had language concerns in Canada; in Belgium there are three main languages: French, Flemish and German. Well supposedly Flemish/Flanders is not really Dutch and Wallonia is not really French. In Brussels street signs are in both Flanders and Wallonia. At least knowing a bit of French helped us. We walked through Parc de Bruxelles with its many busts and manicured vines. Just outside Musée de la Dynastie around the corner from the Old England Building, we spotted a gaufres van and of course had to try a Belgium waffle. Checked out the Musée Royaux des Beaux-Arts park dedicated to women. We made our way to the lower city enjoying the varied architecture. A nice mix of old and older and a bit of new. There were many markets set up in the squares.

We found Chez Léon (which had a favourable write up in Lonely Planet and kids eat free) just down the street from Galerie de la Reine, the covered market hall . Right off the bat we weren't too pleased with our waiter - a busy man yet we're not so sure what he was busy with. The popular meal apparently is "moules et frites" (mussels and french fries). It was amazing how many ways restaurants can promote Mus et Frites - painted on windows, neon, chalkboards, traditional signs. So we gave that a go along with fondu au fromage, boulettes braxelloises and a few different types of Belgium beers. We asked the waiter for a glass of water for Kerala. He brought her a bottle of water already opened. Of course we weren't too keen on this guy and if we were going to pay for a beverage Kerala might as well have had a soft drink, so Brenda said to him not a bottle, water from a tap. Oh you should have seen his face, very disgusted and when Brenda still insisted he (we're not too sure he may have even stomped his foot) said "I'll drink it" and made a big production out of it. It wasn't until later that evening when we were reading the Lonely Planet that read "If you order water in a restaurant, it'll automatically be costly mineral water - brace yourself for a look of contempt if you summon ordinary tap water". By the way very interesting to see a poster in the washroom of "Canada's Chip 'n Dales"?

We then made our way to the Grand Place. Definitely Grand, very impressive with its baroque guildhalls. We rubbed the arm of the statue of Everard 't Serclaes for good luck and read the pledge of brewers. We had a look at a comic mural but feel bad as we're not cult followers of Tintin & Hergé. Lace, lace and more lace.

We then headed down the street to where there were crowds at a corner and yes, we saw the Manneken Pis. Yes, a little bronze statue of a boy peeing with water spurting out. From now on when we think of Belgium this is of what we will be reminded. Along the boulevard leading up to the statue are banners of this statue, most souvenirs are of Manneken Pis, a bar is named after it. A store next door specializes in Manneken Pis and has mannequins of his 650-odd costumes - they dress him up in. You know how they have these billboards that have a picture of a character and then the face is cut out so you go behind and poke your head through? Well this store had one of Manneken Pis but it wasn't only the face that was cut out. People would go behind the board and stick their head and hand (for aiming) through the holes. However not our darling daughter she goes behind and sticks her head out where the hand should be. She certainly got the laughs of other tourists. Our next mission was to recognize Belgium's Equal Opportunity so we searched for Jeanneke Pis. Indeed we found her down an alley squatting. Also, unfortunately, on our way we saw Realman Pis. What's with this urinating in public? Does it have anything to do with having these bathroom attendants and people not wanting to pay?

We checked out the view from the top of a parking garage - apparently the best view of the city. It's hard to believe that there's a river that's been covered over somewhere below Brussels. We made our way back to the hotel at first enjoying the chiming of the church bells but after a few minutes of yelling over them we had had enough. Back at the hotel Kerala enjoyed having a bath - our Denmark apartment does not have a bathtub. We all made it an early night.

Sunday, February 9:

From our hotel room we had a good view of the European Parliament building (the locals call it the Titanic) and the atomium which is a 102 metre high model of an iron molecule at the site of the 1958 World's Fair.

We walked to a square which had a market and had french fries at Maison Antoine, a kiosk which apparently serves the best frites - not bad, similar to Hutch's fries however without the sand and seagulls annoying you. We checked out the dinosaur statues outside the Musée des Sciences naturelles de Belgique. We walked around the huge glass EU building appreciating the many flags. We left Brussels around 1:30 pm and made our way to the town of Ypres.

Ypres (Flanders Fields)

Ypres, which is in Flanders, is the site of many World War I battlefields. We didn't go in the museum which is named "In Flanders Fields..." however we did go to Essex Farm where we saw the remnants of the first aid bunker where Canadian John McCrae wrote the poem. There's a Canadian Heritage plaque just like the ones you see at National Historic sites around Canada with the poem written out. We went to Lakenhalle's for information about the area and asked about the Festival of the Cats - you can buy tickets (very popular with the Japanese) to see cats thrown from the belfry of the hall, well it used to be live cats but now it's toy cats. We made a donation for some British poppies for next Remembrance Day. We visited Meensepoort (the Menin Gate) a huge white gate with the inscription of 54,896 names of British and Commonwealth soldiers who were missing in action, the number of names, and that's just missing, was daunting. We then went in search of the cemetery that our book had a picture of the crosses row upon row however no one knew of it, we think it's really in France. However the cemeteries (French and British) that we did visit are just as moving with their headstones row upon row. We went to the Canadian St Juliaan memorial which is also known as the Brooding Soldier. The monument is very effective. We signed the guest book which is tucked in a box with a door to find that there had been 3 other entries that day. By then the sun had gone down so we made our way to the town of Bruges.

Bruges

Bruges is "Europe's best-preserved medieval city". Rumour has it that the reason that Bruges was not bombed during World War II is because Hitler liked it so much and gave orders that anyone ruining it would be executed. We stayed at the Novotel Hotel right inside the walled city. Besides the walls the city also has a moat around it and many canals running through it. We unpacked and then made our way along the narrow cobblestone streets (very noisy when cars drive over) to the town square - rather nice. We had dinner and then went to a variety store to buy Belgian beer and chocolate. We did not however purchase the corresponding beer glasses. Back at the hotel we had a little party.

Monday, February 10:

After breakfast (included) at which we over-dosed on half descent croissants (something that we've been depraved of in Denmark) we did our own walking tour of the city. Unfortunately Mondays are not the days to visit Bruges as everything, well mostly everything was closed including the town hall tower which provides a great view. Of course, you had to pay an attendant at the washrooms at the tourist information centre. In one Church we saw Michelangelo's Madonna and Child statue and in another we saw the container that has some of Jesus' blood in it. All the windows are hung with lace curtains. We wonder if that's a town by-law.

We went out to the windmills just outside the gate and saw a barge going down the canal. Purchased some high class beer from a brewery. Roamed through a nunnery. Admired the squares; the Markt and the Burg. Criss crossed canals. And yes there were a number of chocolate shops. Here it was February and we found the place enchanting - can't imagine how beautiful it must be in the summer but then you probably wouldn't be able to enjoy because of all the tourists. Very pleasing. At the end of the day we went to a CarreFour and spent € 32.71 ($54.91 Cdn) on Belgian beer and chocolates. We then drove to Roermond which is in the south of Holland. There we stayed at the Theatre Hotel Orangerie.
